    Section 3 Basic Properties of Inequality

    The goal of this project is to formalize elementary properties of inequality in the natural numbers.

    Subsection 3.1 Background

    Similar to divisibility, inequality is a binary relation. For our purposes, we will use the following definition of \(\leq\text{.}\)

    Definition 3.1.

    Assume \(a\) and \(b\) are natural numbers. We say \(a\) is less than or equal to \(b\) and write \(a \leq b\) if there exists a natural number \(c\) such that \(b = a + c\text{.}\)
    In this case, we commonly refer to \(a\) as a lower bound for \(b\) or, symmetrically, to \(b\) as an upper bound for \(a\text{.}\)
    As with divisibility, we have the following standard statements.

    Lemma 3.2. Reflexive.

    For all \(a \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) \(a \leq a\text{.}\)

    Lemma 3.3. Transitive.

    For all \(a,b,c \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) if \(a \leq b\) and \(b \leq c\text{,}\) then \(a \leq c\text{.}\)

    Lemma 3.4. Antisymmetric.

    For all \(a,b \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) if \(a \leq b\) and \(b \leq a\text{,}\) then \(a = b\text{.}\)

    Lemma 3.5. Additive.

    For all \(a,b,c \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) if \(a \leq b\text{,}\) then \(a + c \leq b + c\text{.}\)
    Unlike divisibility, the natural numbers \(a\) and \(b\) are always related by inequality: either \(a \leq b\) or \(b \leq c\text{.}\) When working through proofs, we often want to take the larger of two numbers, which we call the maximum. In Lean, the maximum of a b : \mathbb{N} is max a b. Your goal is to prove the following characterization of the maximum.

    Theorem 3.6.

    Assume \(a,b \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Then \(u \in \mathbb{N}\) is the maximum of \(a\) and \(b\) if and only if \(a \leq u\)and \(b \leq u\text{.}\)
